# Local Minimum

*Draft — 2026-03-21*

---

There are ten people on a plane. The pilot's unconscious.

Nine of them believe they could land it. Nine of them are frozen in their seats.

The tenth doesn't think he can land it. He just gets up.

---

I run a small software company. One product, no investors, no employees. Five years in, it pays the bills and then some. People ask me how I got here and I never have a satisfying answer, because the truth is boring:

I made a decision. Then another one. Most of them were wrong. I corrected them. Kept going.

That's it. That's the whole strategy.

---

In optimization theory, there's this concept of a local minimum — a good-enough solution you find by moving downhill from wherever you're standing. It's not the best possible answer. That's the global minimum, the theoretical optimum you'd find with infinite time and perfect information.

Nobody has infinite time. Nobody has perfect information. The global minimum is a fantasy.

A local minimum is real. It exists because you moved.

---

I used to work in teams. Meetings, mostly. And meetings have this pattern where a decision needs to be made and everyone discusses it in circles. Not because the problem is complicated — because deciding creates accountability, and accountability is risk, and risk is how careers end.

So the meeting loops. Options are weighed. Concerns are raised. Edge cases are explored. And forty-five minutes later, someone says "let's revisit this next week" and everyone nods, relieved.

Nothing was decided. Nothing was risked. Nothing was built.

I'd sit there thinking: *pick one. Any one. The most correctable one. Pick it and move.*

---

Here's what I learned, and it took me longer than it should have:

**A decision you can correct is worth more than a decision you're still making.**

Movement creates information. You learn the terrain by walking it. The map was drawn by someone who walked a different path — it's wrong in exactly the ways you need it to be right.

The failure mode isn't choosing wrong. The failure mode is not choosing.

---

My product was never optimized. It was *moved*. I built a thing, shipped it, listened to what broke, fixed it, shipped again. Five years of local minima, one after another, each one slightly better than the last.

Customers tell me they tried alternatives and kept coming back. Not because I found the perfect answer. Because I found a real one, and then another real one, and then another.

A sequence of local minima, connected by the willingness to move, looks a lot like a global minimum from the outside.

---

The tenth person lands the plane in a field. It's not pretty. Everyone walks away.

The other nine will have opinions about the landing.

He's already walking toward the next thing.
