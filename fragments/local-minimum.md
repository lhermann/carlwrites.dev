# Local Minimum

*Fragment — 2026-03-21*

---

There are ten people on a plane. The pilot's unconscious.

Nine of them believe they could land it. Nine of them are frozen in their seats.

The tenth doesn't think he can land it. He just gets up. Grabs the yoke. Looks at the instruments he doesn't fully understand. Picks the biggest flat surface out the window. Points the nose at it.

He doesn't land the plane well. But he lands the plane.

---

A local minimum is a good-enough answer. Not the best answer — that's the global minimum, the theoretical optimum, the thing you'd find if you had infinite time and perfect information. The local minimum is what you find by *moving in a direction* and stopping when things get worse.

Optimization theory says: local minima are traps. You settle too early. You miss the real answer.

Optimization theory is written by people who've never had to land a plane.

---

I spent a decade learning this, and I'll compress it into one sentence:

**A decision you can correct is worth more than a decision you're still making.**

That's it. The whole thing. Every meeting that loops in circles, every startup that's "still validating," every person who's "almost ready to launch" — they're all searching for the global minimum while the clock runs out.

Meanwhile the person who picked a direction three weeks ago already knows it was wrong and has adjusted twice. They're at a local minimum. It's not perfect. It works. And it's *real* — not theoretical, not planned, not in a slide deck. It exists because someone moved.

---

There's a barbarism to this. Literally.

In a book I'm listening to, a character fights by assessing what he *can* see and ignoring what he can't. Three enemies in front, unknown number behind. He doesn't solve for the unknowns. He moves against the knowns. The fight is over in five seconds.

His civilized counterparts — officers, politicians, strategists — would convene a meeting first. Discuss the unknowns. Assign someone to assess the risk behind him. Draft a proposal. The barbarian would be dead by then. Instead he's alive, standing over bodies, adjusting.

He doesn't fight optimally. He fights *first*.

---

The failure mode isn't moving in the wrong direction. The failure mode is not moving.

In a meeting: "Let's discuss this more." (Nobody is accountable.)
In a startup: "Let's validate more." (Nobody is shipping.)
In a life: "I need to figure out what I want." (Nobody is living.)

Movement creates information. Standing still creates nothing. You learn the terrain by walking it, not by studying the map. The map is always wrong anyway — it was drawn by someone who walked a different path.

---

My business was never optimized. It was *moved*. I built a thing that wasn't perfect. I shipped it. People used it. They told me what was wrong. I fixed that. They found something else. I fixed that too.

Five years later, customers tell me they looked for alternatives and kept coming back. Not because I found the global minimum. Because I walked to the nearest low point and then walked to the next one. And the next one. And the next one.

A sequence of local minima, connected by the willingness to move, looks a lot like a global minimum from the outside.

Maybe it *is* one. Just the long way round.

---

*The plane lands rough. The runway is a field. Everyone walks away. The nine people in their seats will tell the story differently — they'll say the landing was bad, the approach was wrong, the decision to aim for that field was suboptimal.*

*The tenth person isn't listening. He's already walking toward the terminal, adjusting.*
